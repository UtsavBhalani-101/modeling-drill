You just nailed the exact philosophy of Site Reliability Engineering, and you explicitly called out the fundamental weakness of all predictive models: "I am assuming that the new crash will be similar to the crash happened already."If an alien attacks, your model fails. That is the correct, mature understanding of historical data. You cannot model Black Swans. You can only model history.Your logic flow is 90% perfect.Filter the noise: Remove the holidays (Applying the Boundary retroactively).Find the buffer: Look at the 60 minutes before the crash (Finding the Leading Indicator).Set the guard: Alert when the system enters that pre-crash state.But there is a mathematical flaw in your execution that will get you fired.The Fatal Flaw: Never Use "Average"You wrote: "Analyze all these crashes and find the average... try to find a general consensus for this also using average."Averages lie to engineers.Imagine your system crashed three times last year due to memory saturation.Crash 1: Died at 98% Memory.Crash 2: Died at 96% Memory.Crash 3: Died at 70% Memory (a weird memory-leak edge case).The Average crash threshold is 88%.If you set your alert at 88%, what happens when Crash 3 repeats? The server dies at 70%, your alert never fires, and you are staring at a broken system while your dashboard says "Everything is fine."The Professional Calculation: Percentiles and MinimumsWhen querying historical data to find a threshold, you do not look for the average. You use two specific mathematical limits.Method 1: The "Top-Down" Limit (Looking at Death)Instead of the average of the crashes, you look for the Minimum Fatal Threshold.The Query: "What is the lowest metric level that ever resulted in a crash?"The Logic: If the system once died at 85%, then 85% is lethal. It doesn't matter if it usually survives until 95%. You must set your threshold below the lowest known point of failure (e.g., 80%).Method 2: The "Bottom-Up" Limit (Looking at Normal)Sometimes you don't have enough crash data. (If your system is good, it rarely crashes). So you can't measure death. You have to measure Normal.The Query: You look at 30 days of perfectly healthy data (excluding holidays). You calculate the 99th Percentile ($P_{99}$).The Logic: The $P_{99}$ tells you: "99% of the time, our memory usage is below 65%." Therefore, anything above 65% is mathematically abnormal.The Threshold: You set the alert at $P_{99} + \text{Buffer}$ (e.g., 70%).
